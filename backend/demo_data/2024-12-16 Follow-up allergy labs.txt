December 16, 2024

More blood work results trickling in. The C1 esterase inhibitor functional assay came back at 62%. Normal range is 70-130%. That's below normal. The antigen test came back at 18 mg/dL when normal is 21-39 mg/dL. Also low.

So both the quantity and the function of my C1 esterase inhibitor are reduced. That's the classic lab pattern for Type 1 hereditary angioedema — you don't make enough of the protein, and what you do make doesn't work as well as it should.

Dr. Patel called me personally to discuss the results instead of just posting them to the portal. That's when you know it's significant. She was very kind but direct: the lab pattern is consistent with HAE Type 1. She wants to do genetic testing to confirm, but based on the clinical picture (the swelling episodes, the abdominal pain, the low C4, the low C1 inhibitor), she's confident enough to start talking about treatment.

I asked the question I'd been sitting on: is this inherited? She said yes, HAE is autosomal dominant, which means each child of an affected person has a 50% chance of having it. That hit me hard. My mom's "weird swelling episodes" that nobody ever diagnosed. My brother gets random abdominal pain that nobody can figure out. This might explain things going back a generation or more.

Treatment wise, there are medications that can prevent attacks — C1 inhibitor concentrate infusions, or newer drugs like lanadelumab or berotralstat. She also prescribed an emergency treatment (icatibant) that I can carry with me in case of a severe attack, especially if the swelling ever affects my throat.

I'm feeling a lot of things. Relief that there's a name for what's been happening — I'm not crazy, it's not "just stress" or "just allergies." Grief that this is a lifelong condition. Anxiety about the cost of treatment. Gratitude that Dr. Martinez connected the dots and sent me to Dr. Patel.

One step at a time.
