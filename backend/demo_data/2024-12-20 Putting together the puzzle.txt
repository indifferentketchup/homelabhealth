December 20, 2024

I've spent the last few days processing the HAE diagnosis and reading everything I can find. It's strange — I feel like I'm looking back at my entire medical history through a new lens.

All those ER visits for "unexplained abdominal pain" that were always diagnosed as possible IBS or possible food poisoning. The time in college when my face swelled up so bad I couldn't open one eye and the campus clinic just gave me Benadryl (which of course didn't work because HAE swelling isn't histamine-mediated). My mom's episodes that she always said were "just something that happens in our family."

I found a great patient community online. The HAE Association has support groups and educational materials. Reading other people's stories made me feel so much less alone. One woman described exactly what I experience — the prodrome, that weird tingling sensation before the swelling starts. I never knew there was a word for that.

Dr. Patel's office called to schedule the genetic test for January. It'll take a few weeks for results but honestly I don't need it for validation — the labs are pretty convincing. The genetic confirmation is more about understanding which mutation I have, because that can affect treatment options.

I started keeping a symptom journal (hello, this is it). Tracking when swelling happens, what I ate, stress levels, anything that might be a trigger. Common triggers for HAE include stress, minor trauma (even dental work), hormonal changes, and certain medications like ACE inhibitors. I've been on lisinopril for borderline hypertension — and guess what, ACE inhibitors are contraindicated in HAE because they can trigger attacks. Dr. Patel is switching me to something else.

I also need to tell my brother to get tested. That's going to be a hard conversation.

For now, I'm trying to focus on what I can control: learning my triggers, getting on the right preventive treatment, carrying my emergency medication. The diagnosis isn't a death sentence — HAE is very manageable now with the right medications. I just wish someone had figured this out years ago instead of me going through all those ER visits and misdiagnoses.
